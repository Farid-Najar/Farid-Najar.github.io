Details about the M.Sc. “Mathematics and Artificial Intelligence”

The 2-year M.Sc. (also called PhD track) “Mathematics and Artificial Intelligence” is a high level, flexible choice curriculum introducing students to the mathematical tools and theories used in the field of machine learning and artificial intelligence. Its goal is to bring students up to date with current research and train them to push the frontier of knowledge in this rapidly developing field. The curriculum includes modules covering the fundamentals of M.Sc.-level mathematics as well as specialized modules. Particular emphasis is placed on probabilistic and statistical tools, which play a central role. The interaction of these methods with other fields of mathematics (such as optimization, spectral theory, discrete geometry or functional analysis), as well as with computer science, is also of major importance and makes for the richness of this discipline: this is reflected in the choice of proposed modules. The intended goal for this M.Sc. is to prepare students for Ph.D. studies in academia or the private sector.

Courses chosen for the first year

    First semester :

  • Probability theory (Institut de Mathématiques d'Orsay) by Edouard Maurel-Segala and Nathanael Enriquez :

    • Probability space, random variables, distributions, Dynkin system theorem, independence, Kolmogorov's zero–one law, convergence, gaussian vectors, Galton–Watson process, martingale, Markov chains.

  • Mathematics for AI 1 (Institut de Mathématiques d'Orsay) by Gilles Blanchard :

    • Introduction to decision theory, linear classification methods, introduction to statistical machine learning, kernel methods.

  • Deep learning (ENS Paris-Saclay, Centrale-Supélec) by Vincent Lepetit :

    • History of deep learning and its relation to cognitive science, "feedforward networks, regularisation and optimization", representation learning & siamese networks, GAN & transfer learning, recurrent networks and LSTM for Natural Language Processing, object detection, self-supervised methods, deep reinforcement learning.

  • Advanced algorithmic (LRI Paris-Saclay, Centrale-Supélec) by Marc-Antoine Weisser
    and Arpad Rimmel :

    • Complexity theory, polynomial reduction, approximation, polynomial-time approximation scheme, heuristic.


    Second semester :

  • Statistics (Institut de Mathématiques d'Orsay) by Elisabeth Gassiat and
    Thanh Mai Pham Ngoc :

    • Decision theory, estimation, tests.

  • Mathematical optimization (ENS Paris-Saclay) by Alain Trouvé :

    • Gradient descent, Newton methods, optimization under constraints, duality, proximal algorithms,
    weak topology.

  • Mathematics for AI 2 (Institut de Mathématiques d'Orsay) by Christophe Giraud :

    • Mathematical bases of reinforcement and unsupervised learning.

  • Randomized algorithms and games (LRI Paris-Saclay) by J. Tomasik and J. Cohen :

    • Interpretation of the results of a randomized algorithm, heuristic algorithms, heuristic acceleration technics, online algorithms, introduction to game theory.
